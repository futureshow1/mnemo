# 05. Masters of memory — a gallery

The art of memory has its legends. Each figure represents an approach, a technique, an era. Knowing their biographies is a good way to understand that **everything is possible** — and that everyone started from zero.

---

## Simonides of Ceos (~556 – ~468 BCE)

Greek lyric poet from the island of Ceos. According to tradition he discovered the **method of loci** after the collapse of Scopas's banquet hall (see section 01). The first to consciously formulate the principle: **memory rests on space**.

Cicero writes of him: *"Simonides, who first brought this learning..."* (*De Oratore* II.86). Whether or not the anecdote is historical, Simonides functions in the tradition as the mythical father of mnemonics.

## Cicero (Marcus Tullius Cicero, 106–43 BCE)

Rome's greatest orator. In *De Oratore* (II.86–88) and his rhetorical treatises he systematically discusses *memoria* as one of the five canons of speech. Thanks to him the method of loci became an official educational discipline of the Roman world. Memory was for him the **"treasury of all things"** (*thesaurum rerum omnium*).

## Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274)

Dominican, theologian, author of the *Summa Theologica*. In II–II, q.49, a.1 he treats memory as part of the virtue *prudentia* and brings the rules of *Ad Herennium* into the canon of ecclesiastical teaching.

Aquinas gives four mnemonic principles:
1. Convert abstract into concrete image.
2. Order (*ordo*).
3. Concentration and attention.
4. Frequent meditation ("medytacja").

Medieval monasteries built their "palaces" on these principles to remember psalms and tracts.

## Giulio Camillo (~1480–1544)

Italian philosopher and Kabbalist. Created **L'Idea del Teatro** — a physical wooden construction in the form of an inverted amphitheatre. Inside, on seven levels (planets) and seven verticals (sephiroth), he placed hundreds of symbolic images representing the totality of knowledge.

He demonstrated it to King Francis I of France, who funded him. Camillo never finished the work or wrote down a complete key. Only descriptions survive — and the fascination of later generations.

Camillo is the symbol of the dream: that **a spatial map exists in which the entire knowledge of the world can fit**. Wikipedias and knowledge graphs are his children.

## Giordano Bruno (1548–1600)

Italian philosopher, ex-Dominican, burned at the stake by the Inquisition. The most radical figure in the history of the art of memory. Built memory wheels combining astrological, kabbalistic, and alchemical symbols. For Bruno mnemonics was not just technique — it was a path to **the divine mind**.

A figure crucial for understanding why the Renaissance treated memory as a sacred matter. **See the dedicated monograph in section 13.**

## Robert Fludd (1574–1637)

English physician, hermeticist, compiler. In *Utriusque Cosmi Historia* he published detailed engravings of a **memory theatre** — with a Roman arch as the principal *locus*, planets as regions, zodiac signs as places. His diagrams still circulate in books on esoteric Renaissance.

## Matteo Ricci (1552–1610)

Italian Jesuit, missionary in China from 1582. To win the respect of Chinese scholars he mastered Confucian characters using the classical Roman method of loci. He wrote his own mnemonic manual for his students (*Xiguo jifa*, "Western Mnemonic Method", 1596).

His biography was described by Jonathan D. Spence in *The Memory Palace of Matteo Ricci* (1984) — a popular book showing the memory palace in a cross-cultural intellectual context.

## Solomon Shereshevsky (1886–1958)

Russian journalist of Polish-Jewish origin, patient of the famous neuropsychologist Alexander Luria. He had **synaesthesia** — words triggered colours, sounds, tastes, shapes. He remembered everything: tables of numbers years later, random sequences, every conversation.

And — paradoxically — he could barely function socially or professionally. Every word triggered a cascade of images; he could not think abstractly or forget the unnecessary.

Luria described him in **The Mind of a Mnemonist** (1968) — a classic showing that **perfect memory would be a curse**, not a blessing.

## Akira Haraguchi (b. 1946)

Japanese retired engineer. On **3 October 2006** he recited **100,000 digits of π** in 16 hours (with sleep breaks). In 2015 he claimed to know 111,700.

His method: he converted each digit to a kana syllable (Japanese syllabary) and from the syllables built **stories**. The first 15 digits of π in his system are: *"Saishi ikokuni mukosan kowakunaku"* — "The wife and children have gone abroad; the husband is not afraid". The whole π is for him an epic narrative.

Haraguchi shows that techniques **can be invented on one's own cultural ground** — there is no need to use the same systems as the English.

## Chao Lu (b. 1982)

Chinese student. On **20 November 2005** he set the official Guinness record: **67,890 digits of π** without error in 24 hours.

His method: each pair of digits (00–99) was assigned an image (chair, horse, king). From the images he constructed stories tied to physical locations. Classic PAO + memory palace, executed scrupulously.

## Piotr Woźniak (b. 1962)

**Poland's contribution to global learning science.** Computer-science student at AMU in Poznań. In 1985 he begins working on a flashcard system with optimal intervals — first on paper, then (1987) in Pascal.

His **SM-2 algorithm** (1987) underlies most current spaced-repetition apps. Later versions: SM-4, SM-8, SM-15, SM-17, SM-18.

Woźniak develops the **SuperMemo** program — flagship to this day. Author of vast essays on learning, sleep, creativity, free education (his site supermemo.guru). A niche figure but enormously influential: without Woźniak there would be no Anki, no RemNote, no millions of people learning daily with SR flashcards.

## Tony Buzan (1942–2019)

British populariser, author of more than 80 books (*Use Your Head*, *The Mind Map Book*, *The Memory Book*). Inventor and evangelist of **mind maps**. Founder of the **World Memory Championships** in 1991.

Sceptics accused him of commercialization and exaggerated claims. Even so, his contribution to popularizing mnemonics in the second half of the 20th century is enormous. Without Buzan there would be no memory sport in its current form.

## Dominic O'Brien (b. 1957)

Briton, **8-time world champion** (1991, 1993, 1995, 1996, 1997, 1999, 2000, 2001). Author of the **Dominic** system for numbers and cards (see section 04) and books *How to Develop a Perfect Memory*, *Quantum Memory Power*.

A famous record: he memorized **2,808 cards (54 decks)** after a single viewing of each card (2002). For years the face of memory sport.

## Ben Pridmore (b. 1976)

British accountant, **3-time world champion** (2004, 2008, 2009). Created the **Ben System** — an alternative to PAO in which each **triple of digits** (000–999) has one image (1,000 images to learn). The largest mnemonic system in active use.

## Alex Mullen (b. 1991)

American physician, **3-time world champion** (2015, 2016, 2017). The first person to memorize a 52-card deck in **less than 17 seconds**. Active populariser (mullenmemory.com), trains medical and high-school students.

## Joshua Foer (b. 1982)

American journalist. He was writing a reportage on memory championships — and fell down the rabbit hole. After a year of training he won the **USA Memory Championship 2006**. The book **Moonwalking with Einstein** (2011) became a bestseller and popularised mnemonics for millions.

Central thesis: **we are not exceptional. We are merely trained.**

## Boris Konrad (b. 1984)

German of Polish origin, neuroscientist at Radboud University. Combines a memory-sport career with research on the brains of memory champions. Co-author of high-profile *Neuron* (2017) publications showing that mnemonic training reshapes functional brain activation (see section 02).

## Vishvaa Rajakumar (b. 2003)

Indian engineering student, **world champion 2025** (Mumbai), 5,582 points. The first individual title for an Indian competitor. A symbol of the globalization of memory sport — in the 21st century champions come from China, Mongolia, India.

---

## Lessons from the gallery

1. **You don't have to be born a master.** Foer went from zero to USA champion in a year. Not genius — systematic practice.
2. **Each had a personal system.** Major, Dominic, PAO, Ben, Haraguchi — all built their own tools. So can you.
3. **The tradition is alive.** From Simonides to Rajakumar — 2,500 years of the same, refined technique.
4. **The Polish contribution is real.** Without Piotr Woźniak there would be no spaced repetition in its current form.
5. **Mastery is not magic.** It is training — daily 30 minutes for several years. That is all.
