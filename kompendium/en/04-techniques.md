# 04. Classical and modern techniques

## A. Classical mnemonic techniques

### Memory palace (method of loci)

The oldest and most powerful mnemonic technique. Choose a familiar space (childhood home, route to work, a video-game world), set a path with 10–30 stopping points (*loci*), at each point place an imagined image representing what you want to remember. To recall, take a mental walk along the path.

**How to build your first palace.**

1. **Choose a space** you know "with eyes closed". A childhood home is best.
2. **Plan a path** — exactly the same every time. Front door → hallway → kitchen sink → kitchen table → living room sofa → TV → corridor → bathroom → bedroom bed → bedroom window.
3. **Walk it several times in your mind** until automatic. A one-time investment.
4. **Place images.** Convert each list item into a vivid, strange, dynamic image and "set" it at the next locus. The image must *interact* with the place.

**Example.** Shopping list: bread, milk, eggs, butter, salt, oranges, coffee.

- Door: a loaf of bread the size of the door blocks entry; you must bite it.
- Hallway: from the lamp instead of light flows milk.
- Kitchen sink: eggs grow from the tap like coral.
- Kitchen table: butter slicks the chairs into ice-rink slides.
- Sofa: a giant salt shaker sits on it, breathing softly.
- TV: a rain of oranges falls across the screen.
- Corridor: a stream of hot, steaming coffee runs along the floor.

After one walk-through, you remember everything. Test it.

**Variants.** Multiple palaces (one per subject). Expansion (subdivide rooms). Digital palaces (game maps — Skyrim, Witcher). Custom-built sculpted palaces (memory athletes design these).

**When it works best.** Lists, sequences, procedure steps, public speeches (point order), vocabulary, historical dates. Harder for mathematical abstractions — but feasible if you give them an image.

### Link method (chain of associations)

Simpler than the palace, good for beginners. Convert a list into a chain of absurd images, each linking to the next.

**Example — seven deadly sins** (pride, greed, envy, wrath, lust, gluttony, sloth):

A peacock (pride — peacock-tail) holds a bag of coins (greed). From the bag jumps a green envious monster (envy) clutching at the heart of an angry, blazing dancer (wrath) in an open dress (lust). The dancer falls into a giant bowl vomiting stew (gluttony). The bowl cracks and out crawls a sleeping bear (sloth).

Strength: easy. Weakness: a missing link breaks the chain. The palace lacks this flaw (loci are independent).

### Acronyms and acrostics

**Acronym** — a word from the first letters: ROY G BIV (rainbow colours), HOMES (Great Lakes — Huron, Ontario, Michigan, Erie, Superior).

**Acrostic** — a sentence whose word-initials match the list: "**M**y **V**ery **E**ducated **M**other **J**ust **S**erved **U**s **N**oodles" — Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune.

A classic, simple, surprisingly durable.

### Rhymes and rhythm

Rhymes work because they pair the verbal channel with a rhythmic, almost musical one. The brain loves rhythm — one of the oldest organizing systems (oral cultures preserved vast mythological corpora in verse).

- "Thirty days hath September..."
- "I before E except after C..."

When designing your own, **try to make it rhyme and have rhythm**. Harder — but more durable.

### Peg system (number-rhyme, number-shape)

A once-built list of "hooks" matched to numbers. New lists "hang" on the hooks.

**Number-rhyme (English):** 1 = bun, 2 = shoe, 3 = tree, 4 = door, 5 = hive, 6 = sticks, 7 = heaven, 8 = gate, 9 = wine, 10 = hen.

**Number-shape:** 1 = pencil, 2 = swan, 3 = inverted heart, 4 = sailboat, 5 = snake/hook, 6 = lollipop, 7 = flag, 8 = snowman, 9 = balloon, 0 = ball.

Number-shape is language-independent.

### Major system

The most powerful technique for numbers. Each digit is mapped to one or two consonants. Combining them with vowels makes words you visualize.

| Digit | Consonants |
|---|---|
| 0 | s, z |
| 1 | t, d |
| 2 | n |
| 3 | m |
| 4 | r |
| 5 | l |
| 6 | sh, ch, j |
| 7 | k, g |
| 8 | f, v |
| 9 | p, b |

**314** → m, t, r → MOTOR or MITRE. Pick the strongest image.

After learning 0–99 (a one-time week of work), you can memorize any number: split into pairs of digits, convert each into an image, link as a sequence (best in a palace).

This is the system Akira Haraguchi used to memorize 100,000 digits of π.

### Dominic system

Created by eight-time world champion Dominic O'Brien. Specifically for **long sequences of numbers and cards**.

Each digit (0–9) maps to a letter (1=A, 2=B, 3=C, 4=D, 5=E, 6=S, 7=G, 8=H, 9=N, 0=O). Each pair of digits (00–99) maps to a **specific person** with those initials + a **characteristic action**.

- 13 → A.C. → Albert Einstein → writing equation
- 27 → B.G. → Bill Gates → clicking laptop

To remember 1327 → person 13 (Einstein) performs action 27 (clicks laptop). One image = 4 digits.

### PAO system (Person-Action-Object)

The top system in memory sport. For each number 00–99 (or each card in a deck) you build a triple: person + action + object.

To memorize 6 digits: take **person from the first pair, action from the second, object from the third**. 234701 → Madonna (23) shoots (47) a guitar (01). One image = 6 digits.

For a full 52-card deck: 17 images (each linking 3 cards), placed in a palace.

A week to build the system, a month to make it reflex. Then it is yours for life.

### Linkword (keyword method) — for vocabulary

Created by Michael Gruneberg in the 1970s. For learning foreign words.

For a new word: 1. Foreign word + meaning. 2. Find a "key" — a similar-sounding native word. 3. Build a strange image linking the key with the meaning.

**Example.** German *Raupe* (caterpillar) sounds like English "rope". Image: a giant caterpillar climbing a rope into the garage, honking a horn.

Spanish *pato* (duck) sounds like "potty". Image: a duck wearing a yellow potty as a hat.

Boost in vocabulary retention: ~9% on average — but subjectively often much more, because the words **don't evaporate** in a week.

Gabriel Wyner in *Fluent Forever* extends with image cards (no native-language translation, only foreign word + image).

### Mind maps (Tony Buzan)

Visual note-taking. Central topic in the middle, branches radiating outward, each branch a sub-topic, each sub-branch a detail. Use **colours, icons, curved lines, key words** (not full sentences).

Why it works: combines verbal + visual channels, forces hierarchy and structure, exploits radial association, leaves a spatial scaffold.

When to use: lecture notes, essay planning, chapter summary, brainstorm, exam prep (one map = one topic).

When **not** to use: strictly linear material (program code, recipe), when you need a verbatim quote.

## B. Modern learning methods

### Spaced Repetition

**Anki** (open source), **SuperMemo** (Polish original by Piotr Woźniak, 1985), **RemNote**, **Quizlet**, **Memrise**. The SM-2 algorithm and its successors compute the optimal review interval based on your performance ratings.

Optimal session: 15–30 min daily. Better **every day 15 min** than **once a week 2 hours**.

### Active Recall

Instead of reading/highlighting/copying — **force the brain to retrieve from memory**. Each attempt, successful or not, strengthens the trace.

- Close the book after a chapter and write everything you remember. Check.
- Flashcards — try to answer first.
- Self-quizzes.
- Teach someone.
- "Mind dump" — write everything you know on topic X without notes.

Roediger & Karpicke (2006): the testing group remembered **+50%** more after a week than the rereading group.

### Feynman Technique

Four steps: 1. Pick a concept. 2. Explain it in plain language as if to a child (write or speak aloud). 3. Notice where you stumble — those are real gaps. 4. Go back to source, simplify again. Repeat.

Why it works: the brain is prone to "illusion of knowing" — we feel we understand until we try to explain. Feynman exposes this immediately. Bonus: simple explanation **links new knowledge to the existing meaning network**, dramatically strengthening retention.

### Interleaving

Alternating problem types. Subjectively harder; long-term results 50–125% better. A "desirable difficulty".

### Elaborative Interrogation

After each fact, ask **why is it so?**. Generating your own explanation roughly doubles retention.

### Self-Explanation

After each step of reasoning, **explain it to yourself**. In math: after each line write "what I did and why this move".

### Dual Coding

Word + image. Text + diagram. Lecture + mind map. Formula + visualization of its action.

### Pomodoro and ultradian cycles

Pomodoro (Cirillo, 1980): 25/5 work/break, after 4 cycles a 15–30 min break. The brain works in ~90-minute ultradian cycles. Don't push past with no break — it does not pay off.

In breaks: **leave the screen**, walk, look into the distance, drink water. No scrolling — that is not a break for the brain.

### Cornell Note-Taking

Page divided: right column = live notes, left column (after class) = key terms / questions, bottom = brief summary. Reviews: cover the right column, recall from the left (active recall).

## C. Combining everything

Memory champions don't use a single technique — they combine:

- **PAO + palace** = standard kit for memorizing decks of cards.
- **Major + storytelling** = number sequences via stories.
- **Linkword + mind maps** = foreign language acquisition.
- **Palace + spaced repetition** = long-term retention of exam material.

Learn the compendium once. Build practice over years.
