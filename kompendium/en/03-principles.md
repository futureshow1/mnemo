# 03. Twelve principles of effective memorization

Every mnemonic technique ever invented rests on a small set of principles. Learn them once, and you will recognize them in every technique — and learn to design your own.

## 1. Visualization

The brain remembers images far better than words. The **picture superiority effect** — confirmed in hundreds of studies. The brain dedicates ~30% of cortex to visual processing.

Practical rule: do not learn with words, learn with images. The more **vivid, strange, moving, colourful, absurd** — the better. The classical *Ad Herennium* speaks of *imagines agentes* — "acting" images, engaging, almost comic.

Example: instead of memorizing "buy milk", picture a cow dressed as a ballerina waltzing through your kitchen with a milk carton.

## 2. Association

New information "sticks" only when it has something to attach to. The brain is a network of connections; an isolated fact will be swept away.

Each new fact, attach immediately to something already known. The more connections, the more durable the trace. This is the principle of **elaboration**.

## 3. Spatial location (loci)

Spatial memory is phylogenetically the oldest. The hippocampus evolved chiefly to orient an animal in space. Hence the power of the **method of loci**: when you place information in an imagined location, you "hang" it on the strongest hook the brain has.

This principle powers: memory palaces, the Roman room, mind maps, flipcharts, wall posters.

## 4. Multisensory encoding (Dual Coding)

**Allan Paivio** (1971) showed the brain encodes in two parallel channels: **verbal** and **imagistic**. Information encoded in both — e.g. the word "elephant" + the image of an elephant — is remembered **twice as well**.

Extension: add sound, taste, smell, touch, body movement. The more senses, the more durable.

Practice: when learning *amaro* (Italian for "bitter") — imagine the taste of bitter lemon peel, its smell, your grimace, the word spoken aloud. A five-channel trace.

## 5. Emotion

The hippocampus is a neighbour of the **amygdala** — the centre of emotion. Emotionally charged information leaves a stronger trace. That is why we remember where we were on 9/11 (the **flashbulb memory** effect), but not what we ate two Thursdays ago.

In mnemonics: **add emotion to images**. Make them funny, terrifying, disgusting, erotic (Ad Herennium openly recommends images of bodies and violence — because they hit the brain hard). A neutral image dies.

## 6. Narrative (storytelling)

The brain evolved as a story-telling machine. Facts organized as narrative are remembered **about 7× better** than the same facts as a list (Stanford CTL).

In Bower & Clark's experiment (1969) students learned 12 lists of 10 words. The group that wove each list into a story remembered **93%**; the control group **13%**.

Practice: even from three facts try to make a one-sentence story.

## 7. Active retrieval (testing effect)

Each act of recall strengthens the trace more than rereading. Known as the **testing effect** or **retrieval practice**.

In Roediger and Karpicke's experiment (2006): one group read a passage 4 times, another read once and tested itself 3 times. After 5 minutes the reading group did slightly better. **After a week the testing group remembered 50% more.**

Practice: instead of highlighting and rereading — **close the book and tell what you remember**. Check what is missing. Repeat.

## 8. Spaced repetition

Distributed learning beats massed ("cramming"). The **spacing effect** — a meta-analysis of 184 studies (Cepeda et al., 2006) showed distributed learning yields **10–30%** better than condensed.

Optimal intervals expand: first repetition the same day, next in 2 days, then a week, a month, a quarter. The more repetitions, the longer the gaps.

Practice: **Anki** and **SuperMemo** automate this. The SM-2 algorithm (by Polish researcher Piotr Woźniak, 1985) underlies most current systems.

## 9. Interleaving

Alternating different problem types gives better transfer than blocking one type at a time. Counterintuitive — immediate results are lower — but **long-term results are 50–125% better**.

Rohrer & Pashler (2007): students learning geometry. The "blocked" group solved 12 type-A, 12 type-B, 12 type-C. The "interleaved" group alternated. Test a week later: interleaved — **63%**, blocked — **20%**.

Practice: do not study only algebra for two hours. Mix algebra with geometry with trig. Mix vocabulary across semantic fields. The brain learns to **distinguish problem types**, not just an algorithm.

## 10. Depth of processing

**Craik and Lockhart (1972)** — information processed deeply is remembered better than processed shallowly.

- Shallow: "how many syllables?", "what is the first letter?"
- Deep: "what does it mean?", "how does it relate to my life?", "what are examples?", "what are consequences?"

Rule: ask yourself **why**, **how**, **what links to what**. Not wasted time — that *is* learning.

## 11. Chunking

George Miller (1956): consciousness handles **7±2 elements** at once. To hold more, group.

- 1492178919452001 → 1492-1789-1945-2001 (four historical dates)
- A set of words → an acronym (NATO, USA, RAM)
- A set of rules → a hierarchical schema

Experts in their fields see "chunks" the layman misses. A chess master sees 7 *configurations*, not 32 separate pieces.

## 12. Elaboration

Linking new information to **existing knowledge**. Don't learn "Pi = 3.14159..." as a symbol; tie to a concrete (length of a garden hose, age of grandfather, PIN code). The denser the network, the easier future retrieval.

**Elaborative interrogation** — a technique built on the principle: after each fact, ask **why is it so?**. Generating your own explanation doubles retention.

---

## How to read this list

These twelve principles are the **core**. Every technique in sections 04 and 05 is a combination:

- **Memory palace** = location + visualization + association + emotion (often narrative).
- **Spaced repetition** = active retrieval + distributed practice.
- **Mind maps** = visualization + location + chunking + elaboration.
- **Feynman technique** = active retrieval + depth of processing + elaboration.
- **Major system** = visualization + association + chunking.

When you know the principles, you stop learning "tricks" — you begin to **design your own techniques** suited to whatever you want to remember.
