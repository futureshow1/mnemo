# 01. A history of the art of memory

## The founding myth: Simonides of Ceos (5th c. BCE)

According to Cicero (*De Oratore*, II.86) and Quintilian (*Institutio Oratoria*, XI.2), the Greek poet Simonides of Ceos attended a banquet at the home of the Thessalian aristocrat Scopas. He recited a praise poem for his host but devoted half of it to the Dioscuri — Castor and Pollux. Scopas, offended, paid him only half. "Get the rest from the gods," he said.

Moments later a messenger called Simonides outside: two young men were waiting. The poet stepped out — and at that very second the dining hall's roof collapsed, killing every guest. The bodies were so crushed that relatives could not identify them.

Simonides discovered then that he remembered exactly where each guest had sat. He closed his eyes, walked the table in his mind, and pointed each family to its dead.

From this experience, tradition says, the art of memory was born. Two principles emerge from the story and form the core of every classical mnemonic:

1. **Location** (*loci*) — memory is anchored in space.
2. **Images** (*imagines*) — information becomes durable when given visual form.

## The three Roman treatises

In the Hellenistic and Roman period the art of memory became one of the five canons of rhetoric (*memoria*, alongside *inventio*, *dispositio*, *elocutio*, *pronuntiatio*). Three texts mark its mature form:

- **Anonymous, *Rhetorica ad Herennium*** (~90 BCE) — the oldest surviving detailed manual. Long attributed to Cicero. Describes the method of loci with directions for choosing places and constructing *imagines agentes* — vivid, strange, emotional, charged with action.
- **Cicero, *De Oratore* II.86–88** (55 BCE) — transmits the Simonides story and reflects on memory as the "treasury of all things".
- **Quintilian, *Institutio Oratoria* XI.2** (~95 CE) — the most systematic exposition. Distinguishes natural memory (*memoria naturalis*) from artificial memory (*memoria artificialis*). Provides practical exercises.

## The Middle Ages: memory as virtue

In the Middle Ages the art of memory is absorbed into theology and ethics. It serves not only the orator — it becomes part of the virtue of *prudentia* (prudence).

- **Albertus Magnus** (13th c.) and his pupil **Thomas Aquinas** in the *Summa Theologica* (II–II, q.49, a.1) systematize the rules: memory must be trained through repetition, through unusual things, through order, through emotional engagement. Aquinas cites *Ad Herennium* and brings it into the canon of monastic teaching.
- In monasteries monks construct elaborate "palaces" to remember psalms, theological tracts, sermons. **Mnemonic abecedaria** appear — picture alphabets used to catechize the illiterate.

## The Renaissance: memory theatres

The Renaissance brought the most spectacular — and most occult — forms of the art.

- **Giulio Camillo** (~1480–1544) designed **L'Idea del Teatro** — a wooden amphitheatre-shaped construction in whose successive segments were placed images representing the entire order of knowledge: from divine ideas through planets, elements, to virtues and arts. Walking inside, the user was meant to grasp "the totality of reality" at a glance. Camillo demonstrated it to King Francis I of France; only descriptions survive.
- **Giordano Bruno** (1548–1600), burned at the stake for heresy, in *De Umbris Idearum* (1582) and *Ars Memoriae* fused the art of memory with hermeticism, kabbalah, and magic. His memory wheels were meant not so much to help one remember as to give access to the structure of reality. (See the dedicated monograph in section 13.)
- **Robert Fludd** (1574–1637), English hermeticist, in *Utriusque Cosmi Historia* published detailed engravings of a "memory theatre" modelled on Camillo, with a Roman arch as the principal *locus*.
- **Matteo Ricci** (1552–1610), Jesuit missionary in China, used the method of loci to teach Confucian characters to Chinese scholars. His *Memory Palace of Matteo Ricci* was famously described by Jonathan D. Spence.

In this period the art of memory ceases to be merely a technique — it becomes a philosophy: a key to understanding how the mind mirrors the universe.

## Decline (17th–20th c.)

Several forces weakened the art of memory:

- **Print** — from Gutenberg (1455) it became ever easier to "carry memory outside oneself". External memory of the book displaced internal memory.
- **Cartesianism** — rationalism prized analysis over image; pictorial memory began to look childish, "magical".
- **Enlightenment** — pedagogy emphasized understanding over remembering.
- **19th–20th c.** — universal schooling drove rote learning. Memory in education became a duty, not an art.

The art survived in niches: in memorist stage acts, vaudeville, and "memory acts" of magicians.

## Contemporary revival

In recent decades the discipline has returned:

- **Frances A. Yates**, *The Art of Memory* (1966) — a landmark monograph that restored the art of memory as a legitimate field of intellectual history.
- **Tony Buzan** (1942–2019) — popularizer of mind maps, founder of the **World Memory Championships** in 1991.
- 1990s and 2000s — memory sport stars: Dominic O'Brien (8× world champion), Ben Pridmore, Andi Bell.
- **Joshua Foer**, *Moonwalking with Einstein* (2011) — bestseller; reportage by a journalist who in one year went from beginner to USA champion.
- **Polish Piotr Woźniak** in 1985 created **SuperMemo** — the first digital implementation of spaced repetition. The SM-2 algorithm still underlies **Anki** and most flashcard learning apps.
- 2025 — world champion: **Vishvaa Rajakumar** (India), 5,582 points, in Mumbai.

## What this teaches us

1. Effective memorization techniques **have existed for over 2,500 years** and are a deeply human, cultural invention — not a technological one.
2. Civilizations with strong mnemonic traditions (Greece, Rome, medieval monasteries, Renaissance academies) treated memory as part of intellectual and ethical formation, not just a technical skill.
3. That "we cannot remember" is not our trait — it is a consequence of abandoning the tradition. Returning to it (with scientific rigour), we recover something forgotten over the last four centuries.
