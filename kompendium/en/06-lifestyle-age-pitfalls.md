# 06. Lifestyle, age groups, pitfalls and myths

## A. Lifestyle that supports memory

The best mnemonic techniques will not work on a tired, sleep-deprived, stressed brain. This section is about the **foundation** beneath every technique.

### Sleep — memory's number-one partner

What happens to memory in sleep:
1. **Declarative consolidation** in slow-wave (NREM 3) — hippocampus "replays" the day's experience and ships it to the cortex.
2. **Procedural consolidation** in REM — motor and emotional skills.
3. **Creative reorganization** in REM — new combinations of unrelated information.
4. **Metabolic cleansing** — the glymphatic system flushes toxic metabolites (including beta-amyloid).

Consequences of sleep deprivation: 24 h without sleep ≈ 0.10% blood alcohol cognitive function. One night of short sleep (4–5 h) → **~40% less** new-information memorization next day. Chronic shortage = compounding damage, raised dementia risk.

Practice: 7–9 hours, regular bedtime, 18°C cool room, no screens 1 h before bed, no caffeine 6+ h before bed, no alcohol in evening (suppresses REM). A 20–60 min nap improves learning ~20%.

### Aerobic exercise — the first multiplier

Among non-pharmacological interventions, aerobic exercise has the strongest impact on memory and cognition.

Mechanisms: raises **BDNF**, increases brain blood flow, stimulates **hippocampal neurogenesis** (Erickson et al., 2011 — 12 months of aerobic exercise = ~2% hippocampal volume increase), better sleep, reduces stress.

Dose: **150 min/week** moderate (brisk walk, swim, bike) or **75 min/week** intense — WHO minimum. Combined aerobic + strength is best. Even a **10-min walk before study** measurably improves session memory.

### Diet — fuel for memory

**MIND Diet** (a Mediterranean / DASH hybrid). Studies show ~50% reduction in Alzheimer's risk with strict adherence, ~35% with moderate.

Eat: leafy greens daily, other vegetables daily, **berries** (especially blueberries) 2×/week, nuts daily, legumes 4+×/week, whole grains daily, **fatty fish** (salmon, mackerel — omega-3) 1+×/week, poultry 2+×/week, **olive oil** as main fat.

Avoid: red meat, butter/margarine, cheese, sweets, fried/fast food.

Key components: omega-3 (DHA, EPA), polyphenols (berries, olive, coffee, tea, dark chocolate), B12, folate, vitamin D, creatine. Hydration: 2–3 L/day.

### Stress and cortisol — most underrated enemy

Short stress mobilizes (eustress). Chronic stress **damages the hippocampus** — cortisol inhibits neurogenesis and causes neuronal atrophy.

What works (evidence-based): mindfulness meditation (8 weeks of MBSR shows measurable brain changes, lower cortisol, better working memory), yoga, tai chi, walks in nature, breathing techniques (4-7-8, coherent breathing 5-5), sleep, exercise, social support.

### Social relationships and cognitive reserve

People with richer social, intellectual, and educational lives manifest dementia much later — even with the same neuropathology. Networks are more "redundant".

What builds reserve: education, complex cognitive work, multilingualism (bilinguals show dementia ~4–5 years later), active social life, hobbies requiring thinking (chess, instrument, dance, languages).

Social isolation raises dementia risk by ~50%.

## B. Age-group adaptations

### Children (6–12)

**Strengths:** plasticity, learning by imitation, motor and musical memory at peak, language acquisition (peak before 7, drops after 12).

**Weaknesses:** short attention (15–30 min), smaller working memory chunking, less abstract thinking (formal operations stage from ~11).

**What works:** multisensory, memory games (Memory matching, Simon, Concentration), rhymes and songs, mnemonic stories with absurd animals, colour-coded categories, embodied associations (dance, gesture during learning), short sessions / frequent breaks (15 min learn / 5 min movement), positive reinforcement.

**Avoid:** long desk sessions, rote without context, criticism for errors, overly complex systems (Major, PAO — too early).

**First mnemonic:** a mini-palace in the child's room (5–7 loci). A list of favourite toys, family, animals — in absurd poses on furniture. A 7-year-old usually grasps the principle in 10 minutes and is delighted.

### Pupils (13–18)

**Strengths:** abstraction emerging, openness, energy when engaged, still high plasticity.

**Challenges:** information overload (smartphone), short attention without engagement, exam stress, untaught study methods.

**Strategic toolkit:** mini-palaces for dates / definitions / formulas (one palace per subject, ~20 loci); **Anki/SuperMemo** — KEY for matriculation, especially biology, chemistry, languages, history. Daily 20 min, starting 6+ months ahead. Mind maps, Cornell notes, active recall, interleaving, Linkword for languages, Pomodoro 25/5, sleep 8–9 h.

**Avoid:** night cramming before exam, only passive reading, neglecting movement and sleep, multitasking (study + Instagram = zero retention).

### University students (18–25)

**Strengths:** mature working memory and concentration, ability to build long-term knowledge structures, abstract reasoning, time (in theory).

**Challenges:** vast volumes of material (medicine, law, engineering), exam-session stress, parties / sleep deprivation / coffee instead of food, choice between understanding vs. memorizing.

**Strategic toolkit:** **SuperMemo / Anki at full scale** (medicine, law cases, languages — daily 30–60 min); palaces for anatomy, pharmacology; Major or PAO for stats/dates/codes; Feynman for understanding (teach concepts to peers); interleaving; digital notes + knowledge graphs (Obsidian, RemNote, Roam); active group learning; sleep, exercise, MIND diet.

### Working adults (25–50)

**Strengths:** rich context (easier elaboration), better metacognition, discipline.

**Challenges:** no time, work/family stress, fragmented attention.

**Common goals:** foreign language, professional competence, names, presentations without notes, intellectual hobbies.

**Strategic toolkit:** **micro-sessions Anki** (10 min commute), **Linkword** for vocabulary (20 new words/day → ~7,000 in a year, B2 advanced), palace for presentations (15 points in 15 loci of your apartment — speak without notes), **Face-Feature-Link** for names (most distinctive facial feature + image of the name + connection), audiobooks in transit, Cornell notes in meetings, sport / sleep / MIND diet.

### Seniors (50+)

**Strengths:** vocabulary, wisdom, time, often persistent.

**Reality:** gradual atrophy (~5% volume per decade after 40), reduced BDNF, slowed neurogenesis. **Inactivity = accelerated decline.**

**What works (well-evidenced):** aerobic exercise (the strongest intervention against decline — 150 min/week walks, swimming, dance), learning new skills (musical instrument, language, dance, painting), group mnemonic training, social community, reading and writing (journal, demanding books), MIND diet, sleep / blood-pressure / hearing care (Lancet Commission 2020 — modifiable risk factors), computer brain training (with caution: BrainHQ has the strongest evidence; Lumosity skeptically — FTC settlement 2016), spaced repetition (Anki works for seniors too).

Concrete daily practices: 30 min walk minimum, 5 new foreign words/day in Anki, new hobby once a year, daily cognitive task (sudoku, crossword, chess, bridge), contact with at least one person daily.

**Myth to dispel:** "old brain doesn't learn". It does — slower, but it does, until the end of life. Hippocampal neurogenesis in 80-year-olds was confirmed in studies (Spalding et al., 2013).

## C. Pitfalls and myths

**Myth 1: "We use only 10% of the brain."** False. fMRI/PET show activity across virtually all areas. Different regions activate for different tasks — but in total we use 100%.

**Myth 2: Photographic memory is common.** False. True eidetic memory is very rare (mostly children, fading by adolescence). Memory champions don't "photograph" — they encode symbolically.

**Myth 3: Brain training apps raise intelligence.** False in the strong sense. Apps improve performance **on the apps' tasks** (near transfer). No solid evidence for far transfer to real life. Lumosity paid a $50M FTC settlement (2016) for false dementia claims. Better: Anki + real skills + aerobic exercise.

**Myth 4: Left brain = analytical, right brain = creative.** False. Lateralization exists in narrow sense (language). All higher functions engage both hemispheres in complex networks.

**Myth 5: Learning styles (visual/auditory/kinesthetic) are key.** False. Meta-analyses (Pashler et al., 2008; Newton, 2015) found no evidence that matching to style improves outcomes. **Everyone** learns better in multisensory mode (dual coding).

**Myth 6: Cramming the night before works.** Short-term yes; long-term destroys. No sleep = no consolidation. Material crammed disappears in a week.

**Myth 7: "I just have a bad memory."** Mostly false. "Bad memory" usually means: no attention training (shallow encoding), no strategy (cramming instead of imaging), sleep deprivation / chronic stress, lack of interest, or — sometimes — untreated depression, vitamin B12 deficiency, hyperthyroidism. Real genetic memory disorders are <1% of population.

**Myth 8: Coffee "treats" memory.** Half-true. Caffeine improves attention/alertness short-term, may help fresh encoding; doesn't substantially affect consolidation or long-term memory. 2–3 cups a day in the morning are safe and even beneficial for most. Other "smart drugs" (modafinil, amphetamines) — short-term edge, long-term tolerance/dependence/possible harm. **Best smart drugs: sleep, exercise, diet.**

**Myth 9: Mozart raises intelligence.** "Mozart Effect" (Rauscher 1993) showed a small, brief bump in narrow spatial tasks — not in long-term memory or intelligence, not specific to Mozart, not in children. **Playing** an instrument has real long-term effects.

**Myth 10: "Repeat 100 times = learning."** Partially false. Plain repetition has diminishing returns. Better: **active retrieval + spacing + meaningful context**. Mindless rote is the least effective.

**Pitfall 1 — single technique tunnel vision.** Champions combine. Match technique to content.

**Pitfall 2 — waiting for motivation.** Motivation follows action, not the reverse. Set time and place; consistency > intensity.

**Pitfall 3 — "too little to be worth it".** 10 min/day = 60 h/year = enormous gains. Most people do 0.

**Pitfall 4 — flashcards without understanding.** Anki works for **understood** concepts. First understand (Feynman), then consolidate (Anki).

**Pitfall 5 — too complex a system at start.** Start with one technique. Palace on 10 loci. First success. Then expand.

**Pitfall 6 — confusing understanding with remembering.** Understanding is the start. Without active retrieval it fades in weeks.

**Pitfall 7 — ignoring the body.** Mind training disconnected from body is absurd. Without sleep / movement / diet / stress reduction, no technique works at full strength.
