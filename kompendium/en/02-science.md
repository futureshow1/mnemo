# 02. The science of memory

## Three phases: encoding, storage, retrieval

Everything we remember passes through three stages:

1. **Encoding** — the moment information enters the memory system. How well we encode it determines everything else. Shallow encoding ("it was a long word") leaves a weak trace. Deep encoding (linking to image, emotion, meaning, location) leaves a strong one. Craik and Lockhart's classic levels-of-processing theory (1972) showed this experimentally: the same words were remembered better when participants had to judge meaning than when they had to count letters.

2. **Storage** — information must persist hours, days, years. Consolidation occurs mostly in the hippocampus and is shifted to the cortex during sleep. An unused trace decays (the forgetting curve).

3. **Retrieval** — extracting the information. An active, not passive act. Each retrieval modifies the trace — strengthening it (testing effect) or distorting it (reconstruction).

All mnemonic techniques work on encoding. All review methods (spaced repetition, active recall) work on storage and retrieval.

## Types of memory

**Sensory memory** — very brief (milliseconds to 2 seconds) imprint of a stimulus. Iconic (visual), echoic (auditory), haptic (touch). Most of what enters here disappears.

**Short-term / working memory** — capacity about **7±2 units** (George Miller, 1956), duration of seconds without rehearsal. The "workbench" of consciousness. To hold more, we use **chunking** — grouping items. The number 738291465 is hard; 738-291-465 much easier.

**Long-term memory** divides into:

- **Declarative** (explicit, consciously accessible):
  - **Episodic** — events from our lives ("what did you do yesterday at 6?")
  - **Semantic** — knowledge of the world ("the capital of Poland is Warsaw")
- **Non-declarative** (implicit):
  - **Procedural** — skills (riding a bike)
  - **Conditioning** — automatic responses
  - **Priming** — influence of prior stimuli

Mnemonics work mostly on episodic and semantic memory.

## The Ebbinghaus forgetting curve

German psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus ran one of the first memory experiments, 1880–1885. He learned lists of nonsense three-letter syllables (BAW, KEX), then tested how much he remembered after various intervals. The result:

- After **20 min** — ~40% forgotten
- After **1 hour** — ~55% forgotten
- After **1 day** — ~66% forgotten
- After **6 days** — ~75% forgotten
- After **31 days** — ~80% forgotten

The curve is **exponential** — we lose fresh information fastest, then slow down. This is brutal for night-before-the-exam cramming; of what you learned today, ~30% will remain tomorrow.

Ebbinghaus also showed the **rescue**: each repetition, especially just before forgetting, "flattens" the curve. After five planned repetitions the material becomes durable for years. This is the foundation of **spaced repetition**.

## The hippocampus and consolidation

The hippocampus — a structure in the medial temporal lobe — is the centre of new episodic memory formation. Patient **H.M.** (Henry Molaison), whose hippocampi were surgically removed in 1953 for epilepsy, could not form new factual memories for the rest of his life (he died in 2008). Procedural memory worked — he learned new motor skills, though he did not remember learning them.

**Consolidation** is the gradual transfer of trace from hippocampus to cortex. It takes hours, days, months. It happens most strongly during sleep — chiefly in slow-wave (NREM 3) and REM. No sleep = no consolidation.

Crucially: the adult human hippocampus **still produces new neurons** (adult neurogenesis). Long controversial, this has now been confirmed. Activities supporting neurogenesis: aerobic exercise, learning new things, social contact, sleep, reduced stress and alcohol.

## BDNF and neuroplasticity

**BDNF** (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor) — a protein nicknamed "Miracle-Gro for the brain". Supports neuronal growth and survival, formation of new synaptic connections, plasticity.

Raises BDNF: **aerobic exercise** (most strongly studied), sleep, diet rich in omega-3 and polyphenols, learning new skills, intermittent fasting.

Lowers BDNF: chronic stress (cortisol), sleep deprivation, the Western diet (sugar, trans fats), social isolation.

Practical conclusion: **memory training without physical activity works at half strength**. Combining movement, sleep, and mnemonics is not luxury — it is a multiplier.

## Sleep — memory's partner

Three things happen to memory in sleep:

1. **Consolidation** — transfer of traces from hippocampus to cortex.
2. **Synaptic cleansing** — the glymphatic system flushes toxic metabolites (including beta-amyloid). No sleep = their accumulation.
3. **Reorganization** — in REM, memories are recombined; sleep supports **creative insight**.

Practice: 7–9 hours of sleep, regular bedtime, dark and cool room, no screens for an hour before bed. A 20–60 minute nap improves learning by ~20%.

## Why the brain works this way (evolutionary perspective)

Why do we forget? Because remembering everything would be catastrophic. The patient Solomon Shereshevsky (described by Alexander Luria in *The Mind of a Mnemonist*) had quasi-photographic memory — and could barely function. He could not think abstractly because every word triggered an avalanche of images.

**Forgetting is a function, not a bug.** The brain evolved to remember what matters for survival: threats (strong fear emotions), food and its locations (space!), faces (evolutionarily key), stories and characters (group cohesion).

Mnemonics work because **they exploit the brain's natural preferences**: space, images, faces, emotions, narratives. They do not fight the brain — they borrow its strongest modes.

## Why memory champions are not exceptional

A study by Martin Dresler, Boris Konrad and colleagues (Radboud University, *Neuron*, 2017) compared the brains of 23 memory champions with a comparable control group. The result:

- **Brain anatomy** — no difference.
- **IQ** — no difference.
- **Activation during memory tasks** — dramatically different. In champions, areas linked to spatial processing (medial parietal cortex, hippocampus) lit up.
- **After 6 weeks of training**, controls began to activate the same regions — and their results doubled. The effect persisted four months.

Conclusion: **mnemonics is teachable like any skill.** Not talent. Six weeks of daily practice for measurable, lasting changes in brain function.
